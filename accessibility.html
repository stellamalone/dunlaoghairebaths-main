Our Commitment to Accessibility

At Dun Laoghaire Baths, we are deeply committed to ensuring an accessible and inclusive digital environment for all our users, including those with disabilities. Our goal is to provide a seamless, user-friendly experience that allows everyone to engage fully with our content and services. Here's how we plan to achieve and maintain high standards of web accessibility.

Conformance Standards

Our website strives to conform to the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 at Level AA, a set of internationally recognized guidelines for making web content more accessible. These standards help us cater to the needs of users with various disabilities, including visual, auditory, physical, speech, cognitive, language, learning, and neurological disabilities.

Continuous Monitoring and Updating

We are dedicated to continuously monitoring and improving our accessibility features. Our team reviews the accessibility policy annually and after any major website update to ensure our commitment remains steadfast and our practices evolve with technological advancements.

Web Accessibility Audit Plans

Our comprehensive web accessibility audit involves both manual and automated testing methods, alongside input from individuals with disabilities. Automated testing will involve using software tools to scan the website for accessibility issues. These tools can quickly identify a range of problems, such as missing alt text for images, insufficient colour contrast, and missing form labels. However, they are not capable of catching all types of accessibility issues, especially those that require human judgement. Manual testing is performed by individuals who go through the website using various assistive technologies such as screen readers, keyboard-only navigation, and magnification software. This method helps identify issues that automated tools might miss, such as logical flow of content, meaningful sequence of information, and the overall experience of using the site with assistive technologies.This approach helps us pinpoint and understand the barriers within our website. Our audit report includes:

1. A list of accessibility defects:

  • Missing alt text on images;
  • Colour contrast;
  • Non-labelled forms;
  • Lack of keyboard navigation;
  • Videos without captions.

2. Defect explanations

Each identified issue will be described in detail, explaining why it's a problem and how it affects users with disabilities, referencing the specific guideline or criterion from the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) that the defect violates.

3. Instructions on how to locate or duplicate defects

The report will include step-by-step instructions or paths to find each issue within the website.

4. Screenshots or Recordings

Role: Accessibility Auditor Responsibilities:

  • Capture visual evidence of each accessibility defect identified during the audit. This could be in the form of screenshots for static issues or screen recordings for dynamic issues (e.g., issues that occur during navigation or interaction with website elements).
  • Ensure that the evidence clearly illustrates the issue at hand. For example, if a text is not readable due to poor contrast, the screenshot should show the text in its actual context on the website.
  • Annotate the screenshots or recordings with explanations or arrows to highlight the issue, making it easier for the team to understand and locate the defects.

5. Repair Suggestions

Role: Accessibility Expert / Web Developer Responsibilities:

  • Provide detailed recommendations for fixing each identified defect. This includes specific changes to code, design elements, or content.
  • For example, if an issue involves insufficient colour contrast, the recommendation should specify the colour values (hex codes) that would meet the WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) standards for contrast.
  • Offer alternative solutions when applicable, giving the team options to choose from that best align with the website’s branding and design principles.
  • Document these recommendations in a structured format, such as a table or list, aligning them with the corresponding defects for clarity.

6. Defect Prioritisation

Role: Project Manager Responsibilities:

  • Prioritise defects based on their impact on the user experience and compliance with accessibility standards. High-priority issues are those that significantly hinder access to the website’s core functions for users with disabilities.
  • Use a standardised severity rating system (e.g., critical, high, medium, low) to classify defects. Critical issues might include non-navigable websites with keyboard-only input or missing alternative text for crucial images.
  • Coordinate with web developers, designers, and potentially users with disabilities, to agree on the order of importance.
  • Ensure the prioritisation list is shared with all team members and that there is a clear timeline for addressing and retesting each issue according to its priority.

Implementing Accessibility Repairs

Addressing accessibility issues is a collaborative work at Dun Laoghaire Baths. For instance, if our audit reveals the need for better colour contrast, our visual designers propose alternatives that stay true to our brand while enhancing accessibility. Once approved, our developers implement these changes, ensuring our website meets the highest accessibility standards.
